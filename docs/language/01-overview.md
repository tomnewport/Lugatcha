# The Uzbek Language: Overview

## What Uzbek is

Uzbek (native name *oʻzbek tili*, "Uzbek language", or *oʻzbekcha*) is a
**Turkic** language and the **official state language of the Republic of
Uzbekistan**. It is one of the two most widely spoken Turkic languages (alongside
Turkish), with roughly **35 million speakers** — most in Uzbekistan, with
significant communities in Afghanistan, Tajikistan, Kyrgyzstan, Kazakhstan,
Turkmenistan, and a diaspora elsewhere.

Within the Turkic family, Uzbek belongs to the **Karluk (Qarluq)** branch,
making its closest major relative **Uyghur** (spoken in Xinjiang, China). It is
more distantly related to Turkish, Azerbaijani, Kazakh, Kyrgyz, Turkmen, and
Tatar. A Turkish and an Uzbek speaker can pick out shared words and structures
but generally cannot hold a fluent conversation without effort — the
relationship is roughly like that between distant Romance languages.

## What makes it Turkic (the headline features)

If you know one thing about Turkic languages, know that they are
**agglutinative**: grammar is expressed by gluing a chain of suffixes onto a
root, each suffix carrying one piece of meaning. A single Uzbek word can express
what English needs a whole phrase for. The language also has:

- **Vowel harmony** (weaker in Uzbek than in most Turkic languages — see below).
- **No grammatical gender** — no "he/she" distinction in pronouns, no gendered
  nouns or adjectives.
- **No articles** — nothing equivalent to "a" or "the".
- **Subject–Object–Verb (SOV) word order** — the verb comes last.
- **Postpositions** instead of prepositions — they follow the noun.

These are explored in detail in [Grammar](04-grammar.md).

## How Uzbek differs from its Turkic cousins

Uzbek has been heavily shaped by centuries of contact with **Persian (Tajik)**
in the great oasis cities — Samarkand, Bukhara, Tashkent. This contact did two
notable things:

1. **It loaded the vocabulary with Persian and (through Persian) Arabic words.**
   Everyday Uzbek is full of Persian-origin terms — far more than, say, Kazakh.
2. **It eroded vowel harmony.** Most Turkic languages have strict vowel harmony,
   where suffix vowels must match the vowels of the root (front/back, rounded/
   unrounded). Standard literary Uzbek — based on the Persianised speech of the
   cities — largely lost the front/back distinction. This is a real
   simplification for the learner but makes Uzbek the "odd one out" among Turkic
   languages. (Note: many rural and northern dialects *do* retain vowel harmony.)

A second major layer is **Russian**, the legacy of the Tsarist and especially
the **Soviet** period (Uzbekistan was the Uzbek SSR from 1924 to 1991). Russian
contributed administrative, technical, and modern-life vocabulary, and Russian
remains a widely spoken second language and lingua franca, particularly in
Tashkent and among older urban and professional people.

## Dialects and the standard language

Uzbek is dialectally diverse. Linguists usually group the dialects into:

- **Karluk** dialects (the prestige group, including the speech of Tashkent,
  Samarkand, Bukhara, the Ferghana Valley) — the basis of the literary standard.
- **Kipchak** dialects (more vowel harmony, closer to Kazakh, spoken by more
  rural/nomadic-descended populations).
- **Oghuz** dialects (in the south, near Turkmenistan, with Turkmen-like
  features).

The **standard literary language** taught in schools and used in media is based
on the **Tashkent/Ferghana** urban speech. As a learner you should aim for the
standard; be aware that what you hear in a village or in a different region may
diverge in pronunciation and vocabulary.

## Relationship to Russian and to multilingualism

Uzbekistan is genuinely multilingual. You will commonly encounter:

- **Uzbek** — the state language, the language of national identity, and
  increasingly dominant in public life since independence.
- **Russian** — still a lingua franca, the language of much business, science,
  and inter-ethnic communication; common in Tashkent. Knowing some Russian is
  genuinely useful for a visitor.
- **Tajik (Persian)** — the first language of much of the population of
  **Samarkand and Bukhara**, where many people are bilingual in Uzbek and Tajik.
- **Karakalpak** — a distinct Turkic language (closer to Kazakh) and the
  co-official language of the autonomous **Republic of Karakalpakstan** in the
  northwest.
- Smaller communities speak Kazakh, Kyrgyz, Turkmen, Korean (the Koryo-saram),
  and others.

For a visitor or an agent: **Uzbek is the right language to learn for respect
and national identity; Russian is the most useful fallback; in Samarkand and
Bukhara, Tajik is everywhere.**

## A very short history of the language

- **Pre-modern:** The Turkic language of Central Asia developed as **Chagatai**
  (*chigʻatoy*), a literary Turkic language written in the Arabic script,
  flourishing from roughly the 15th century. Its towering figure is the poet
  **Alisher Navoiy** (1441–1501), who argued for and demonstrated the literary
  power of Turkic. Modern Uzbek is the direct heir of Chagatai.
- **Early 20th century:** Reformist intellectuals (the **Jadids**) modernised the
  language and literature before being crushed under Stalin.
- **Soviet period:** The language was standardised and its script changed twice
  by decree — from Arabic to a **Latin** alphabet (1928–29) and then to
  **Cyrillic** (1940). Vocabulary was Russified.
- **Since 1991:** Independent Uzbekistan made Uzbek the sole state language and
  legislated a return to a **Latin** script (1993, revised since). The
  transition has been slow and is still incomplete — which is why both scripts
  coexist today. See [Writing systems](02-writing-systems.md).

## Why this matters for the learner

Three takeaways frame everything else in this guide:

1. **Uzbek is not hard because it is exotic; it is hard because it is built on a
   different blueprint** (agglutination, SOV, suffix-stacking) than English. Once
   the blueprint clicks, it is very regular.
2. **You must deal with two alphabets.** This is a logistical hurdle unique to
   the post-Soviet Turkic world.
3. **Vocabulary is layered** — a Turkic core, a deep Persian/Arabic stratum, and
   a Russian veneer — which is both a challenge (more to learn) and a help
   (Persian and "international" words are sometimes guessable).
