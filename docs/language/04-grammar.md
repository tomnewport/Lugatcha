# Grammar

This is the heart of why Uzbek feels different from English. Uzbek is built on a
fundamentally different plan: it is **agglutinative**, **suffixing**,
**head-final**, and it has **no gender and no articles**. Once you internalise
the plan, the language is strikingly **regular** — far fewer irregular forms than
English, French, or German.

This document gives an honest working overview, not a complete grammar. Examples
use Latin spelling.

## The big picture: agglutination

Uzbek expresses grammar by **stacking suffixes onto a root**, one after another,
each adding one unit of meaning. There is almost no prefixing. Word order within
the suffix chain is fixed and logical.

A famous illustrative chain on the root *ish* ("work"):

```
ish            work (noun)
ishchi         worker            (-chi: agent, "one who does")
ishchilar      workers           (-lar: plural)
ishchilarim    my workers        (-im: 1st person possessive)
ishchilarimiz  our workers       (-imiz: 1st person plural possessive)
```

Or on a verb, the root *kel-* ("come"):

```
kel-          come
kelmoq        to come (the dictionary/infinitive form, -moq)
keldi         (he/she) came
kelmadi       (he/she) did not come         (-ma-: negative)
kelolmadi     (he/she) could not come        (-ol-: ability)
kelolmadingiz you (polite) could not come    (-ngiz: 2nd person polite)
```

The order is consistent: **root + derivation + plural + possessive + case** for
nouns; **root + voice + negation + ability/aspect + tense + person** for verbs.
You read and build words by peeling or adding layers.

## No gender, no articles

- **No grammatical gender.** The pronoun **u** means "he", "she", *and* "it".
  Nouns and adjectives have no gender. This removes a whole category of difficulty
  that French, German, Russian, etc. impose.
- **No definite/indefinite articles.** There is no "the" and no "a". Definiteness
  is inferred from context, word order, and case marking. *Kitob* can mean "a
  book", "the book", or "books" (generic) depending on context. The numeral
  **bir** ("one") can act loosely like "a" when you want to stress
  indefiniteness, but it is not an article.

## Word order: Subject–Object–Verb (SOV)

The default order is **Subject – Object – Verb**, with the **verb at the end**.

```
Men   non    yedim.
I     bread  ate
"I ate bread."
```

More generally Uzbek is **head-final**: modifiers precede what they modify, and
the most important consequence is that **the verb closes the sentence**. Other
consequences:

- **Adjectives precede nouns:** *katta shahar* (big city).
- **Possessor precedes possessed:** *Aziz**ning** kitob**i*** (Aziz's book) —
  note both ends are marked (genitive on the owner, possessive on the thing).
- **Postpositions, not prepositions:** the relational word *follows* the noun.
  *Stol **ustida*** (on the table — literally "table its-top-at"); *men **bilan***
  (with me).
- **Relative clauses and subordinate material come before the main verb,** often
  built with participles and verbal nouns rather than with words like "that" or
  "which". This is one of the harder things to get used to (see
  [Challenges](06-challenges-for-english-speakers.md)).

Word order is somewhat flexible for emphasis (the element right before the verb
is typically in focus), but verb-final is the safe default.

## Vowel harmony (the Uzbek caveat)

In most Turkic languages, suffix vowels change to "harmonise" with the root's
vowels (front vs. back). **Standard literary Uzbek has largely lost front/back
vowel harmony** because of Persian influence — which is a genuine simplification
for the learner. You will mostly use **one form of each suffix** regardless of
the root vowel, where Turkish or Kazakh would alternate.

Caveats to keep honest:

- A weaker **rounding harmony** and some consonant-driven alternations still
  affect a few suffixes.
- Many **dialects (especially Kipchak)** *do* keep full vowel harmony, so you may
  hear it.
- Some suffixes still alternate for other reasons (e.g. voicing: `-da`/`-ta`,
  `-ga`/`-ka`/`-qa`).

Net: vowel harmony is **a much smaller burden in Uzbek than in its cousins**, but
not entirely absent.

## Nouns: number, possession, case

Nouns take suffixes in this order: **plural → possessive → case.**

### Plural: -lar

Add **-lar** for the plural: *kitob* → *kitoblar* (books), *bola* → *bolalar*
(children). Note: after a number, the noun stays **singular**: *uch kitob*
("three book", = three books). The plural marker is not used when a quantity is
already specified.

### Possessive suffixes

Possession is shown on the **possessed noun** with a personal suffix (English
uses separate words "my, your, his"):

| Person | Suffix | Example (*kitob*, book) |
|---|---|---|
| my | -(i)m | kitobim |
| your (sing./informal) | -(i)ng | kitobing |
| his/her/its | -(s)i | kitobi |
| our | -(i)miz | kitobimiz |
| your (pl./polite) | -(i)ngiz | kitobingiz |
| their | -(s)i / -lari | kitobi / kitoblari |

### Case suffixes

Uzbek has **six core cases**. This is the system that does the work English does
with prepositions and word order.

| Case | Suffix | Rough meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nominative** | (none) | subject | *uy* — house |
| **Genitive** | -ning | possession ("of") | *uyning* — of the house |
| **Accusative** | -ni | definite direct object | *uyni* — the house (as object) |
| **Dative** | -ga (-ka/-qa) | to / towards / for | *uyga* — to the house |
| **Locative** | -da | at / in / on | *uyda* — at home / in the house |
| **Ablative** | -dan | from / out of | *uydan* — from the house |

Two points English speakers should note:

- The **accusative -ni** typically marks a **definite/specific** object. An
  indefinite object often takes no case suffix at all. *Kitob oʻqidim* (I read [a]
  book) vs. *Kitobni oʻqidim* (I read **the** book).
- The **dative -ga** is enormously useful and covers "to/for/towards" — direction,
  recipients, purposes.

### Putting it together

*uy* (house) → *uylar* (houses) → *uylarim* (my houses) → *uylarimda* (in my
houses). Root + plural + possessive + case, every time.

## Pronouns

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **men** (I) | **biz** (we) |
| 2nd | **sen** (you, informal) | **siz** (you, polite/plural) |
| 3rd | **u** (he/she/it) | **ular** (they) |

- **sen vs. siz** is a politeness distinction (like French *tu/vous*). **Use
  *siz*** with strangers, elders, and anyone you should respect. *Sen* is for
  close friends, children, intimates — using it wrongly is rude. There is an even
  more deferential plural **sizlar**.
- Because the verb is marked for person, **pronouns are often dropped** when
  clear from context (a "pro-drop" language). *Keldim* already means "I came".

## Verbs

Verbs are where agglutination shines. The dictionary form ends in **-moq**
(*kelmoq* "to come", *bormoq* "to go", *yozmoq* "to write"). The root is the
form without -moq (*kel-*, *bor-*, *yoz-*).

### Verb suffix order

Roughly: **root + voice (causative/passive/reflexive/reciprocal) + negation +
modality (ability) + tense/aspect + person/number.**

### Negation: -ma-

Insert **-ma-** right after the root: *keldi* (came) → *kelmadi* (did not come);
*bilaman* (I know) → *bilmayman* (I do not know).

### Person endings

Verbs agree with the subject. The endings differ a little by tense, but the
personal markers are recognisable: *-man/-m* (I), *-san/-ng* (you), *-di/-∅*
(he/she), *-miz* (we), *-siz/-ngiz* (you pl./polite), *-lar/-di* (they).

### Core tenses/aspects (a practical subset)

| Form | Marker | Meaning | Example (*kel-*, come) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Definite past | -di- | did, completed | *keldim* — I came |
| Present-future | -a-/-y- | I do / will do (general) | *kelaman* — I come / will come |
| Present continuous | -yapti / -moqda | am doing now | *kelyapman* — I am coming |
| Indefinite/perfect past | -gan | have done / did (reported) | *kelganman* — I have come |
| Future (intentive) | -moqchi | intend to | *kelmoqchiman* — I intend to come |

Uzbek's tense/aspect system is richer than this — it distinguishes, for example,
**witnessed vs. reported/inferred past** (evidentiality), which English lacks —
but the table above will carry a beginner a long way.

### Modality and ability

- **Ability** ("can"): root + **-(a)ol-** : *kelaolaman* / *kelolaman* (I can
  come); negative *kelolmayman* (I can't come).
- **Necessity** ("must/should"): the construction **-(i)sh kerak** : *borish
  kerak* (it is necessary to go / one must go).

### The verb "to be"

The present "to be" is usually expressed by **person-marked endings** attached to
the predicate, or by the copula clitics:

- *Men talabaman.* — I am a student. (*talaba* + *-man*)
- *U uyda.* — He/she is at home. (zero copula in 3rd person)
- Past "was" uses **edi**: *U uyda edi.* — He was at home.

There is also an existential **bor** ("there is/exists") and its negative **yoʻq**
("there is not"), which also serve to express **possession** (Uzbek has no verb
"to have"):

- *Mening kitobim **bor**.* — I have a book. (lit. "my book exists")
- *Vaqtim **yoʻq**.* — I have no time.

## Adjectives

Adjectives are simple: they **do not agree** with the noun (no gender/number/case
agreement) and they **precede** the noun. *Katta uy* (big house), *katta uylar*
(big houses) — the adjective never changes.

Comparison: comparative with the ablative **-dan** + adjective ("more ... than"):
*Bu undan katta* (this is bigger than that, lit. "this from-that big").
Superlative with **eng**: *eng katta* (the biggest).

## Questions

- **Yes/no questions** are formed with the particle **-mi** attached to the
  predicate: *Keldingmi?* (Did you come?), *Bu kitobmi?* (Is this a book?).
  Intonation also carries questions in speech.
- **Wh-questions** use question words placed (usually) just before the verb:
  *kim* (who), *nima* (what), *qayer/qayerda* (where), *qachon* (when), *nega*
  (why), *qanday/qanaqa* (how/what kind), *qancha/necha* (how much/many).

## Derivation: building words

A lot of vocabulary is **derived transparently** with productive suffixes —
learn these and your effective vocabulary multiplies:

| Suffix | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **-chi** | agent ("one who...") | *ish* work → *ishchi* worker; *futbol* → *futbolchi* footballer |
| **-lik** | abstract noun / "-ness" | *yaxshi* good → *yaxshilik* goodness; *do'st* friend → *do'stlik* friendship |
| **-li** | "having, with" | *suv* water → *suvli* watery; *aql* mind → *aqlli* intelligent |
| **-siz** | "without, -less" | *aqlsiz* mindless; *uysiz* homeless |
| **-xona** | "place/house for" (Persian) | *kasal* sick → *kasalxona* hospital; *oshxona* canteen/kitchen |
| **-ston/-iston** | "land of" (Persian) | *Oʻzbek**iston*** land of the Uzbeks |

The last two are Persian loans, a reminder that Uzbek's grammar is Turkic but its
word-building borrows from Persian too.

## What is genuinely easy here

To balance the difficulty: Uzbek grammar gives the English speaker several
genuine gifts — **no gender, no articles, almost no irregular verbs, regular and
predictable suffixes, weak vowel harmony, and a phonetic spelling.** The
difficulty is in the *re-wiring* (suffix-stacking, verb-final order, thinking in
cases), not in memorising exceptions. See
[Challenges](06-challenges-for-english-speakers.md) for how to prioritise.
