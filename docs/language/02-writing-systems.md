# Writing Systems: The Two-Alphabet Problem

The single most disorienting practical fact about written Uzbek is that it is
written in **two alphabets at once** in everyday life: a **Latin** script
(official) and a **Cyrillic** script (still pervasive). A third script, the
**Arabic** (Perso-Arabic), is historical and still used by Uzbek communities in
Afghanistan. To read modern Uzbekistan you need to recognise both Latin and
Cyrillic. This document teaches you to.

## Why there are two (a quick history)

- Until the 1920s, Uzbek (as literary Chagatai) was written in the **Arabic**
  script.
- **1928–1929:** the Soviets switched Uzbek to a **Latin** alphabet (part of a
  pan-Turkic Latinisation programme).
- **1940:** the Soviets switched it again to **Cyrillic**, aligning it with
  Russian and cutting Central Asia off from Latin-script Turkey.
- **1993, after independence:** Uzbekistan legislated a return to a **Latin**
  alphabet, partly to assert independence and align with Turkey. The 1995 reform
  produced the version in use today; further tweaks have been proposed
  (especially to the awkward `oʻ`, `gʻ`, and digraphs) but adoption is gradual.

The result in 2020s Uzbekistan: **schools, official documents, signage, and
branding increasingly use Latin; but books, older signage, much of the internet,
and many people's habits remain Cyrillic.** Older people often read Cyrillic far
more comfortably. **You cannot get by knowing only one script.**

## The Latin alphabet (official)

The modern Uzbek Latin alphabet has **29 letters** plus the apostrophe-like
*tutuq belgisi*. The unusual part is a handful of letters that do not map
one-to-one to English sounds.

### Letters that behave like English

`a, b, d, e, f, g, h, i, j, k, l, m, n, o, p, q, r, s, t, u, v, x, y, z`

Mostly intuitive for an English reader, **with these exceptions:**

| Letter | Sound | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **j** | "j" as in *jam* (and sometimes "zh" as in *measure* in loanwords) | |
| **q** | a deep "k" made far back in the throat (uvular) | Not English "kw". A real, distinct sound. |
| **x** | "kh" — like the *ch* in Scottish *loch* or German *Bach* | **Not** "ks" and not "z". |
| **y** | "y" as in *yes* (a consonant) | Never a vowel. |
| **o** | depends — see `oʻ` below | |

### The special letters (learn these)

| Letter | Name / form | Sound |
|---|---|---|
| **Oʻ oʻ** | "o" + turned comma | A rounded back vowel, roughly the *aw* in British *more* / the German *o* in *Sonne*. **A different letter from plain `o`.** |
| **Gʻ gʻ** | "g" + turned comma | A voiced uvular fricative — a guttural "gh", like a French/Arabic *r* (*غ*). |
| **Sh sh** | digraph | "sh" as in *shoe*. |
| **Ch ch** | digraph | "ch" as in *church*. |
| **Ng ng** | digraph | "ng" as in *sing*. |
| **ʼ** | *tutuq belgisi* ("apostrophe") | Marks a glottal stop or lengthens a preceding vowel; separates sounds. E.g. *sanʼat* (art). |

> **The turned comma (ʻ) vs. the apostrophe (ʼ) vs. the keyboard `'`.**
> Properly, `oʻ` and `gʻ` use a *modifier letter turned comma* (U+02BB), while
> the glottal-stop mark is a *modifier letter apostrophe* (U+02BC). In real life
> almost everyone types a plain ASCII apostrophe `'` for all of them:
> `o'zbek`, `g'isht`, `san'at`. **Treat `o'` and `oʻ` as the same letter; treat
> `g'` and `gʻ` as the same letter.** Many casual texts and URLs drop the mark
> entirely (`ozbek`), which you must learn to read through.

### A worked example

The word for "Uzbek / Uzbekistan" appears in the wild as all of:

- `Oʻzbekiston` (proper Latin)
- `O'zbekiston` (ASCII apostrophe — most common typed form)
- `Ozbekiston` / `Uzbekiston` (mark dropped / Anglicised)
- `Ўзбекистон` (Cyrillic)

All the same word.

## The Cyrillic alphabet (still everywhere)

Uzbek Cyrillic is the Russian alphabet plus a few extra letters for sounds
Russian lacks. If you know Russian Cyrillic you are most of the way there.

### Full correspondence table

| Cyrillic | Latin | Sound | Cyrillic | Latin | Sound |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| А а | a | a | Р р | r | r |
| Б б | b | b | С с | s | s |
| В в | v | v | Т т | t | t |
| Г г | g | g | У у | u | u |
| Д д | d | d | Ф ф | f | f |
| Е е | e / ye | e, ye | Х х | x | kh |
| Ё ё | yo | yo | Ц ц | ts | ts (loanwords) |
| Ж ж | j | j / zh | Ч ч | ch | ch |
| З з | z | z | Ш ш | sh | sh |
| И и | i | i | Ъ ъ | ʼ | hard sign / glottal |
| Й й | y | y | Ь ь | — | soft sign (loanwords) |
| К к | k | k | Э э | e | e |
| Л л | l | l | Ю ю | yu | yu |
| М м | m | m | Я я | ya | ya |
| Н н | n | n | **Ў ў** | **oʻ** | the `oʻ` vowel |
| О о | o | o | **Қ қ** | **q** | uvular q |
| П п | p | p | **Ғ ғ** | **gʻ** | guttural gh |
| | | | **Ҳ ҳ** | **h** | h |

The four bottom-row letters with tails/marks — **Ў, Қ, Ғ, Ҳ** — are the
Uzbek-specific additions. Note the split that confuses learners: Cyrillic has
**both Х (= Latin x, "kh")** and **Ҳ (= Latin h, plain "h")**, which Latin keeps
distinct as `x` and `h`.

### Reading tip

If you learn to read Cyrillic, you can transliterate almost mechanically into
Latin and vice versa, because the two official scripts are designed to map onto
each other. The mapping is *nearly* one-to-one; the main friction points are the
`ye/yo/yu/ya` iotated vowels and the `х/ҳ` (`x/h`) pair.

## The Arabic (Perso-Arabic) script

The historical script of Chagatai and pre-1928 Uzbek. It is:

- An abjad written **right to left**, derived from Persian/Arabic, with extra
  letters for Turkic sounds.
- Still used by the large **Uzbek community in Afghanistan** and in some
  religious and classical contexts.
- Important for anyone reading **pre-modern literature** (e.g. Navoiy) in the
  original.

A visitor to Uzbekistan today will rarely need it, but it explains the
calligraphy seen on historic monuments (much of which is Arabic-language
Qur'anic text rather than Uzbek).

## Practical guidance

- **Learn to read both Latin and Cyrillic.** It is not optional. Latin for new
  signage, branding, and official text; Cyrillic for books, older signs, and a
  lot of the internet and social media.
- **Expect inconsistency.** A single street may have signs in both. Names of
  the same place are spelled several ways.
- **When typing Uzbek,** the ASCII apostrophe for `o'` and `g'` is universally
  understood; the turned comma is "correct" but optional in practice.
- **For agents processing text:** build a transliteration step. Treat `oʻ`,
  `o'`, `o‘`, and `ў` as equivalent; likewise `gʻ`/`g'`/`ғ`. Normalise before
  matching. Be aware that `x` ≠ `h` and `q` ≠ `k` — these are meaning-bearing
  distinctions, not noise.
