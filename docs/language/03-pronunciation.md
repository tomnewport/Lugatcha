# Pronunciation

Uzbek pronunciation is, for an English speaker, mostly approachable — there are
no tones, no clicks, and spelling is largely phonetic (what you see is what you
say). The difficulties are concentrated in a few consonants made further back in
the throat than English uses, and in a couple of vowels English lacks.

This document uses the **Latin** spelling. See
[Writing systems](02-writing-systems.md) for the Cyrillic equivalents.

## The golden rule: it is (mostly) phonetic

Once you know the value of each letter, you can read Uzbek aloud. There are no
silent letters and few of English's spelling traps. The main caveats are the
script's special letters and some assimilation in fast speech.

## Vowels

Standard literary Uzbek has **six vowels**. (This is the Persianised standard;
some dialects have more and retain front/back harmony — see
[Grammar](04-grammar.md).)

| Letter | Approx. English | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **a** | between *cat* and *father* | An open vowel; in many words closer to "ah". |
| **e** | *bed* | |
| **i** | *bit* / *ski* | |
| **o** | *hot* (British), or the "aw" in *thought* | An open back vowel. |
| **u** | *put* / *boot* | Rounded. |
| **oʻ** (o') | British *more* shortened, German *Sonne* | **The hard one.** A rounded mid-back vowel, distinct from both `o` and `u`. Cyrillic **ў**. |

The key vowel contrasts to drill:

- **o vs. oʻ** — `bor` (there is / go) vs. `boʻr` (chalk). Different words.
- **u vs. oʻ** — `pul` (money) vs. `poʻl` — round your lips for `oʻ` but keep the
  tongue lower than for `u`.

English speakers tend to **diphthongise** vowels (turn a pure "o" into "ow", "e"
into "ey"). Uzbek vowels are **pure and short** — keep them clean and clipped.

## Consonants

Most consonants match English. Focus your effort on these:

| Letter | Sound | How to make it |
|---|---|---|
| **q** | uvular "k" | Like "k" but made as far back as you can, where you'd gargle. Contrast with `k`: `qil` (do/hair) vs. `kil` — and place names like *Qarshi*, *Samarqand*. |
| **gʻ** (g') | voiced uvular fricative | The "gargled r" of French/Arabic (*غ*). A continuous guttural "gh". As in *Gʻijduvon*, *togʻ* (mountain). |
| **x** | voiceless velar/uvular fricative | The *ch* in *loch*, German *Bach*. As in *xat* (letter), *yaxshi* (good). **Not** "ks". |
| **h** | plain English "h" | Breathy. Distinct from `x`. Cyrillic **ҳ**. |
| **r** | tapped/trilled | A Spanish/Italian rolled or tapped "r", not the English glide. |
| **j** | usually "j" (*jam*); "zh" in some loanwords | Context-dependent. |
| **ng** | "ng" as in *sing* | Can occur where English wouldn't expect it. |
| **ʼ** | glottal stop / vowel length | The catch in *uh-oh*; or it just lengthens the previous vowel. *taʼrif* (description), *sanʼat* (art). |

### The three "back of the throat" sounds to master

`q`, `gʻ`, and `x` are the signature Uzbek sounds and the ones an English speaker
most often gets wrong (collapsing them into `k`, `g`, and `h`/`k`). They are
**meaning-bearing** — getting them wrong can change the word. Time spent here
pays off in being understood. A useful contrast set:

- **x** (loch) vs **h** (hat): *xolis* vs *holis*.
- **q** (deep k) vs **k** (front k): *qadr* vs *kadr*.

## Stress

Stress in Uzbek is **light and fairly predictable**, normally falling on the
**last syllable** of a word. It shifts onto the final syllable as suffixes are
added. A few suffixes and particles are unstressed (e.g. the negative `-ma`).

Crucially, **Uzbek stress is much weaker than English stress.** English massively
reduces unstressed vowels (the "uh" schwa in *banana*). Uzbek keeps unstressed
vowels **full and clear**. The single most effective thing an English speaker can
do to sound less foreign is to **stop reducing vowels** — give every vowel its
full value.

## Things English speakers reliably get wrong

1. **Reducing vowels to schwa.** Say every vowel fully (see above).
2. **Diphthongising "o" and "e".** Keep them pure.
3. **Merging q/k, gʻ/g, x/h/k.** Learn the back consonants.
4. **English "r".** Tap or roll it.
5. **Aspirating less, or differently.** Minor, but English over-aspirates
   initial p/t/k.
6. **Mispronouncing `oʻ`** as plain "o" or "oo". It is its own vowel.
7. **Reading `x` as "z" or "ks"** — it is "kh".

## Minimal practice set

Say these cleanly, keeping vowels pure and getting the back consonants right:

- *yaxshi* — good ("yakh-shi")
- *rahmat* — thank you ("rah-mat", plain h)
- *Toshkent* — Tashkent ("tosh-kent")
- *Samarqand* — Samarkand (deep q)
- *togʻ* — mountain (guttural gʻ at the end)
- *oʻzbek* — Uzbek (the `oʻ` vowel)
- *choʻl* — desert/steppe
- *suv* — water
- *non* — bread

If you can produce *oʻzbek*, *togʻ*, *yaxshi*, and *Samarqand* recognisably, you
have the four hardest features of Uzbek pronunciation under control.
