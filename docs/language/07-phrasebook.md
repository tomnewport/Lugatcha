# Phrasebook

A practical survival phrasebook. Each entry gives the **Uzbek (Latin)**, a rough
**pronunciation** in English-friendly spelling, and the **meaning**. Pronunciation
guide: `kh` = the throaty *x* (as in *loch*); `gh` = guttural *gʻ*; `q` = deep
*k*; capital letters mark light stress (usually the last syllable). Default to the
**polite (siz)** forms shown.

> Reminder: a guest is honoured in Uzbek culture. Even a few words of Uzbek,
> attempted respectfully, are received very warmly. See
> [Customs & etiquette](../culture/06-customs-and-etiquette.md).

|---|---|---|

| Vaalaykum assalom | va-a-LAY-kum as-sa-LOM | (reply) and upon you peace |
| Salom | sa-LOM | Hi (informal) |
| Xayr | khayr | Goodbye |
| Ko'rishguncha | ko-rish-GUN-cha | See you later |
| Xush kelibsiz | khush ke-LIB-siz | Welcome |
| Ha / Yo'q | ha / yoq | Yes / No |
| Rahmat (katta rahmat) | rah-MAT | Thank you (thank you very much) |
| Iltimos | il-ti-MOS | Please |
| Marhamat | mar-ha-MAT | Please go ahead / you're welcome / here you are |
| Kechirasiz | ke-chi-ra-SIZ | Excuse me / sorry |
| Mayli | MAY-li | OK / all right |

## How are you?

| Uzbek | Pronunciation | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Yaxshimisiz? | yakh-shi-mi-SIZ | Are you well? / How are you? |
| Qalaysiz? | qa-LAY-siz | How are you? |
| Yaxshi, rahmat | YAKH-shi, rah-MAT | Fine, thank you |
| Ishlaringiz qalay? | ish-la-rin-GIZ qa-LAY | How is your work / how are things? |
| Yaxshi, o'zingizchi? | ...o-zin-GIZ-chi | Good, and you? |

Uzbek greetings are often a **string** of these well-wishes exchanged rapidly;
you are not expected to answer each literally — the exchange itself is the
courtesy.

### The bread rule, in passing

**Non** (bread) is close to sacred. Don't place it upside down, don't put it on
the ground, don't waste or bin it, and tear it by hand rather than cutting it.
See [Cuisine](../culture/05-cuisine.md) and
[Customs](../culture/06-customs-and-etiquette.md).

## A two-line survival kit

If you memorise nothing else, memorise these:

1. **Assalomu alaykum** — opens every interaction respectfully.
2. **Rahmat** — closes it gracefully.

With "hello", "thank you", "please" (*iltimos*), "excuse me" (*kechirasiz*), and
"how much?" (*qancha?*), you can move through a day in Uzbekistan with goodwill on
your side.
