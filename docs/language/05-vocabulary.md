# Vocabulary and Loanwords

Uzbek vocabulary is a layered thing. The **grammar** is solidly Turkic, but the
**words** come from four main strata, and recognising the layers makes learning
much faster — and reveals a lot about the country's history.

## The four layers

1. **Turkic core.** The basic, high-frequency words — body parts, kinship,
   numbers, basic verbs, nature — are native Turkic and often recognisable across
   Turkic languages. *suv* (water), *non* (bread — actually Persian, see below),
   *koʻz* (eye), *bosh* (head), *bormoq* (to go), *kelmoq* (to come), *bir, ikki,
   uch* (one, two, three).

2. **Persian (Tajik) layer.** Centuries of living alongside Persian in the oasis
   cities embedded an enormous number of Persian words in everyday Uzbek — far
   more than in most Turkic languages. These cover culture, abstract ideas,
   household life, food, and more. Examples: *non* (bread), *oshxona* (kitchen),
   *gul* (flower), *dard* (pain), *do'st* (friend), *rang* (colour), *shahar*
   (city), *baxt* (happiness), *dunyo* (world), and the suffixes *-xona*
   (place) and *-iston* (land).

3. **Arabic layer (mostly via Persian).** Arabic words entered with **Islam** and
   classical learning, covering religion, law, science, morality, time, and
   formal/abstract vocabulary. Examples: *kitob* (book), *maktab* (school),
   *ilm* (knowledge/science), *vaqt* (time), *salom* (peace/greeting), *halol*
   (permitted/halal), *insof* (conscience/fairness), *dunyo* (world, Arabic),
   names of the days and months, and most given names.

4. **Russian / "international" layer.** From the Tsarist and especially **Soviet**
   period came administrative, technical, scientific, and modern-life vocabulary,
   plus international words that arrived through Russian. Examples: *stol* (table),
   *poyezd* (train), *mashina* (car/machine), *telefon*, *kompyuter*, *avtobus*,
   *direktor*, *kassa* (cash desk), *remont* (repair/renovation). Many science and
   bureaucratic terms are Russian-shaped.

Since independence there has been a conscious push to **re-Turkicise** or
**re-Persianise** some Soviet-era Russian vocabulary (e.g. preferring *tayyora*/
older terms, or Turkic coinages, over Russian ones), so you will sometimes meet
two words for the same modern concept — an older Russian-derived one and a newer
"purified" one.

## Why this helps the learner

- **Persian and Arabic words are shared across a huge cultural zone.** If you know
  any Persian, Urdu/Hindi, Turkish, or even some Arabic, you will recognise
  chunks of Uzbek vocabulary. *Kitob* (book), *maktab* (school), *dunyo* (world),
  *vaqt* (time), *salom* are familiar far beyond Uzbekistan.
- **"International" words are often guessable** through their Russian forms:
  *universitet, telefon, avtobus, restoran, banan, doktor, muzey, teatr*.
- **Productive Turkic suffixes** (see [Grammar](04-grammar.md)) let you generate
  and decode many words from a known root: *ish* (work) → *ishchi* (worker),
  *ishsiz* (unemployed), *ishlamoq* (to work).

polite speech — see the etiquette guide.

## False friends and traps

- **x** is "kh", not "ks" — *taxi* is not "taksi"; the Uzbek for taxi is *taksi*
  with a `ks` written as **ks**, while *x* in *yaxshi* is the throaty sound.
- **non** means **bread**, not "no" or "none".
- **bor** means "there is / exists" or "go!" — not "bore".
- **ko'p** means "much/many", not "cup".
- **bola** means "child", not "ball".
- **oʻzbek** the people/language; **Oʻzbekiston** the country. Do not call the
  country "Uzbek".

## A note for agents

When normalising or matching Uzbek text:

- Expect the **same concept in two or three lexical forms** (Turkic / Persian /
  Russian), and the **same word in two scripts and several apostrophe styles**.
- Loanword spelling is not always stable (Russian borrowings especially).
- Personal and place names are overwhelmingly **Arabic/Persian in origin**
  (*Abdulla, Aziz, Dilnoza, Sherzod, Gulnora*) and have standard but
  multiply-transliterated spellings.
