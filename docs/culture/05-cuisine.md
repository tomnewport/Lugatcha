# Cuisine

Food is at the centre of Uzbek hospitality and identity. Uzbek cuisine is one of
the richest of Central Asia — hearty, meat- and bread-based, shaped by Silk Road
trade, settled-oasis agriculture, and nomadic Turkic roots. Knowing the dishes
and the rituals around them is essential to being a good guest. (See also
[Daily life](04-daily-life.md) and
[Customs & etiquette](06-customs-and-etiquette.md).)

## The national dish: osh / plov

**Osh** (also **palov / plov / pilaf**) is *the* national dish — a celebratory
rice dish of **rice, mutton or beef, lots of carrots, onions, and oil**, often
with chickpeas, raisins, garlic bulbs, and sometimes quail eggs or *kazy*
(horsemeat sausage). It is:

- Cooked in a giant cast-iron **qozon** (cauldron), traditionally by **men**, and
  the subject of regional pride — **every region has its own style** (Tashkent,
  Samarkand, Ferghana, Bukhara each differ).
- The centre of celebration and ritual: the **nahorgi osh** ("morning plov") is a
  dawn gathering, especially before weddings.
- So culturally important that Uzbek plov is on **UNESCO's Intangible Cultural
  Heritage** list.

If offered plov, accept enthusiastically and praise it. Saying *Juda mazali!*
("very delicious!") delights a host.

## Bread: non — treat it with reverence

**Non** is the round, flat, decorated **tandoor bread**, baked on the wall of a
clay **tandir** oven and central to every meal. **Samarkand non** is especially
famous (dense and long-keeping). Bread carries strong cultural and quasi-sacred
meaning, with firm etiquette:

- **Never place non upside down** — it is considered disrespectful/unlucky.
- **Never put bread on the ground**, step on it, or throw it away; stale bread is
  repurposed, not binned.
- **Tear it by hand**, don't cut it with a knife; the eldest or host often breaks
  it first.
- Breaking bread together seals friendship and agreements.

These rules matter more than almost any other table custom — observe them.

## Tea: choy — the constant

**Choy** (tea) is offered to every guest immediately and drunk all day. Usually
**green tea (*koʻk choy*)** in the hot south, **black tea (*qora choy*)**
elsewhere, served in small handle-less bowls (**piyola**), without milk.

Notable tea rituals:

- The host pours, and there is a custom of **pouring the first cup back into the
  pot two or three times** ("*qaytarish*") to mix and aerate the brew before
  serving.
- Cups are filled **only part-way** as a sign of respect — a small cup means the
  host attends to you often and you stay longer; a full cup can imply "drink up
  and go". Don't be offended by a half-full cup; it's courtesy.
- Accept tea; it is the basic gesture of hospitality. Refusing the first cup can
  seem cold.
- The **choyxona** (teahouse) is a traditional social institution.

## Other dishes you will meet

| Dish | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Shoʻrva (shurpa)** | Hearty meat-and-vegetable soup, often mutton. |
| **Manti** | Large steamed dumplings filled with meat (and onion/pumpkin). Eaten by hand. |
| **Chuchvara** | Small boiled dumplings, often in soup. |
| **Lagʻmon (laghman)** | Hand-pulled noodles with meat and vegetables, in broth or fried. |
| **Somsa (samsa)** | Baked savoury pastry (meat/onion or pumpkin), from the tandoor. |
| **Shashlik (kabob)** | Skewered grilled meat — a street and celebration staple. |
| **Norin / Naryn** | Cold dish of thin noodles with boiled horsemeat — a Tashkent specialty. |
| **Dimlama** | Slow-braised meat and vegetable stew. |
| **Mastava** | Rice soup, sometimes called "liquid plov". |
| **Qovurma / qaynatma** | Various braised/boiled meat dishes. |
| **Achichuk / shakarob** | Fresh salad of thinly sliced tomato and onion — the classic accompaniment to plov. |

## Dairy, sweets, and fruit

- **Dairy:** *qatiq* (thick yoghurt), *suzma* (strained yoghurt/soft cheese),
  *qaymoq* (clotted cream), and *kurt/qurt* (hard dried salty cheese balls, a
  nomadic snack).
- **Sweets and the *dasturxon* spread:** A guest table (*dasturxon*) is laid
  before the meal with **dried fruit and nuts** (apricots, raisins, almonds,
  pistachios, walnuts), **halva**, *navat* (rock sugar), *parvarda* (boiled
  sweets), *chak-chak* (fried dough in honey), and **non**. You graze on these
  with tea before and after the main course.
- **Fruit and melons:** Uzbekistan is famous for its **melons** (*qovun*) and
  **watermelons** (*tarvuz*), grapes, apricots, pomegranates, figs, and cherries —
  genuinely a source of national pride. Praising the melons is a sure way to a
  host's heart.

## Drinks

- **Tea** above all; also **ayran** (salted yoghurt drink), fruit compotes
  (*kompot*), and mineral water.
- **Alcohol:** Despite the Muslim-majority population, the Soviet legacy means
  **alcohol (vodka, beer, local wine and cognac) is available and consumed**,
  especially among men and at celebrations, and Uzbekistan produces wine. But
  attitudes vary: many devout people abstain, and it is **rude to push alcohol on
  someone who declines**. In religious or conservative settings, don't assume
  alcohol is welcome. Toasts (a Russian-influenced custom) are common at feasts.

## Dietary notes for visitors

- **Meat-heavy cuisine:** Mutton/lamb and beef dominate; **horsemeat** features in
  some specialties (*kazy*, norin). Pork is rare (Muslim norms) but present in
  Russian/Korean contexts.
- **Halal** is the default for most food; ask *Halolmi?* if it matters to you.
- **Vegetarians/vegans** will find it challenging — even "vegetable" dishes often
  use meat stock or fat, and plov is built on meat and oil. You can survive on
  bread, salads, dairy, somsa with pumpkin, vegetable lagʻmon, fruit, and nuts,
  but say clearly *Men goʻsht yemayman* ("I don't eat meat") and expect some
  puzzlement.
- **The food is rich and oily** by design (energy-dense, celebratory); pace
  yourself, especially with plov.

## Table manners in brief

- **Wash hands** before eating; meals may begin and end with the gesture of
  cupping the hands and drawing them down the face (**omin/amin**), a brief
  blessing — follow your host's lead.
- **Sit where directed**; the place of honour (**toʻr**) is for elders/honoured
  guests.
- Eat from communal dishes politely, often by hand or with the implements given;
  use the **right hand** for eating and passing food.
- **Don't refuse food and tea outright** — accept, and pace yourself; leaving a
  little is fine, but wasting bread is not.
- **Compliment the food and thank the host**; *Rahmat, toʻydim* ("thank you, I'm
  full") and *Juda mazali* go a long way. *Osh boʻlsin!* is a fitting thing to say
  around a meal.

## For an agent

The cuisine encodes the culture: **plov = celebration and regional identity; non =
near-sacred bread with strict handling rules; choy = the universal ritual of
hospitality; the dasturxon = abundance offered to guests.** If reasoning about
food contexts in Uzbekistan, weight **hospitality obligation, the bread taboos,
halal defaults, and the meat-centric diet** heavily.
