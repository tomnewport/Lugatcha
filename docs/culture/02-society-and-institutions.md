# Society and Institutions

A reference to how Uzbekistan is organised — the state, religion, education,
media, the economy, money, and the practical institutions a visitor or agent
needs to understand. Figures are approximate and change; verify specifics
(exchange rates, office-holders, laws) against current sources.

## The state

- **Official name:** Republic of Uzbekistan (*Oʻzbekiston Respublikasi*).
- **System:** A presidential republic. In practice power is **highly centralised**
  in the **President**, who is head of state and dominates the executive. The
  current president is **Shavkat Mirziyoyev** (since 2016; see
  [History](01-history.md) and verify current status).
- **Legislature:** A bicameral parliament, the **Oliy Majlis** (Supreme Assembly),
  with a **Legislative Chamber** (lower house) and a **Senate** (upper house).
- **Government:** Led by a **Prime Minister**; ministries run the usual portfolios.
- **Administrative divisions:** 12 **provinces** (*viloyat*), the autonomous
  **Republic of Karakalpakstan** (*Qoraqalpogʻiston*) in the northwest, and the
  capital city **Tashkent** as a separate unit. Below that: districts (*tuman*)
  and, at the grassroots, the **mahalla** (see below and
  [Daily life](04-daily-life.md)).
- **Capital:** **Tashkent** (*Toshkent*) — the largest city in Central Asia
  (~3 million), the political, economic, and cultural centre, with a famous metro
  system whose stations are architectural showpieces (photography now permitted).
- **Politics, candidly:** Reforming and opening since 2016, but still an
  **authoritarian-leaning** system with limited political pluralism and press
  freedom. Treat partisan political opinion as sensitive in conversation.

### The mahalla — the most distinctive institution

The **mahalla** is a traditional **neighbourhood community** and, uniquely in
Uzbekistan, a recognised **unit of local self-government**. It organises mutual
aid, life-cycle events (weddings, funerals), dispute resolution, and social
support — and also serves as the lowest tier of administration, with an elected
chairman (*oqsoqol*, "white-beard") and committee. It is central to social life
and is discussed more in [Daily life](04-daily-life.md). For a visitor, the key
point: **community, not just the individual or the state, is the basic social
unit.**

## Religion

- **Majority:** **Islam**, predominantly **Sunni** of the **Hanafi** school, the
  faith of roughly 90% of the population. Islam is woven into culture, calendar,
  food (halal), names, and etiquette — but the practice is generally **moderate
  and culturally embedded** rather than strict, a legacy of both Central Asian
  tradition and Soviet secularisation.
- **Sufism** has deep historical roots here (the **Naqshbandi** order originated
  near Bukhara), and the region's great shrines and Sufi saints remain important.
- **The state is officially secular** and keeps a firm, watchful control over
  religious institutions and expression; "non-traditional" or political Islam is
  restricted.
- **Minorities:** A historic **Orthodox Christian** (largely Russian) community, a
  once-significant **Bukharan Jewish** community (now small after emigration), and
  others. Bukhara and Samarkand have notable Jewish heritage.
- **For a visitor:** Dress and behave **modestly around mosques and shrines**
  (cover shoulders/knees, remove shoes, women may cover the head). Ramadan
  (*Ramazon*) and the two **Eids** (*Hayit*) shape the calendar. See
  [Customs & etiquette](06-customs-and-etiquette.md).

## Education

- A strong cultural value is placed on **education and knowledge** (*ilm*) — a
  heritage stretching back to the medieval madrasas of Bukhara and Samarkand.
- The system: near-universal **literacy** (a Soviet achievement, ~100%),
  compulsory schooling, and a growing **higher-education** sector. The language of
  instruction is increasingly **Uzbek**, with Russian-medium and other streams
  still present; English is rising fast as the prestige foreign language.
- International branch campuses (Westminster, Webster, Inha, and others) operate in
  Tashkent, reflecting the post-2016 opening.

## Media and information

- **Television and print** were long state-dominated; since 2016 there has been
  **partial liberalisation** and a lively growth of **online media, bloggers, and
  social platforms**, though red lines (especially around the president and
  security) remain.
- **Languages of media:** Uzbek and Russian dominate.
- **Connectivity:** Mobile penetration is high; **Telegram** is extremely popular
  for news, chat, and commerce; messaging apps are central to daily communication.

## Economy and money

- **Economy:** A lower-middle-income, **resource- and agriculture-rich** economy.
  Key sectors: **cotton** (historically dominant, now being diversified away from,
  with reforms to end forced labour in the harvest), **gold** (Uzbekistan is a
  major producer — the Muruntau mine), **natural gas**, minerals, textiles,
  **agriculture** (fruit, especially famous melons, apricots, grapes), and a
  fast-growing **tourism** sector. **Remittances** from labour migrants (many
  working in Russia and Kazakhstan) are a significant part of household income.
- **Currency:** the **Uzbek soʻm** (UZS; symbol sometimes *soʻm*). It has **many
  zeros** — historically subject to high inflation, so prices run into the tens
  and hundreds of thousands; large banknotes were introduced to cope. Since the
  **2017 currency liberalisation**, the previously dominant black-market exchange
  has largely converged with the official rate, and money-changing is now normal
  through banks. Carry cash for bazaars and small vendors; **card payments and
  digital wallets** (e.g. local apps, Uzcard/Humo) are widespread in cities.
- **Practical tips:** Many transactions are cash; keep small notes; tipping is
  modest and appreciated (see [Customs & etiquette](06-customs-and-etiquette.md)).

## Health, safety, transport (practical)

- **Safety:** Uzbekistan is generally regarded as a **safe** destination with low
  violent crime; normal urban precautions apply. Police presence is visible.
- **Transport:** The **Tashkent metro**; a modern **high-speed rail** (the
  *Afrosiyob*) linking Tashkent–Samarkand–Bukhara; shared taxis and *marshrutka*
  minibuses; ride-hailing apps in cities. The country is **doubly landlocked**, so
  travel is overland or by air.
- **Time and week:** Time zone **UTC+5**. The work week is generally **Monday–
  Friday/Saturday**; **Sunday** is the main day off. **Friday** (*juma*) is the
  Muslim day of communal prayer but a normal working day.

## Key national symbols and holidays

- **Flag:** Blue, white, and green horizontal bands separated by red fimbriations,
  with a white crescent moon and twelve stars.
- **National holidays of note:** **Navroʻz** (Nowruz, ~21 March — the spring new
  year, a huge, joyous public festival), **Independence Day** (*Mustaqillik kuni*,
  1 September — the biggest state holiday), the two **Eids** (*Ramazon Hayit* and
  *Qurbon Hayit*), **Memorial Day** (9 May, *Xotira va qadrlash kuni*), **Women's
  Day** (8 March), **New Year** (1 January, secular, Russian-style with a
  "Father Frost" tradition), and **Constitution Day** (8 December).

## For an agent: institutions to map

If you need to reason about Uzbekistan's structures, the useful mental model is:
**President-centred state → ministries → provinces (viloyat) + Karakalpakstan +
Tashkent → districts (tuman) → mahalla (community self-government).** Overlay
**Hanafi Sunni Islam under firm state supervision**, a **reforming, opening
economy** built on cotton/gold/gas/tourism and remittances, a **high-inflation-
scarred soʻm** now liberalised, and a **media landscape thawing but still
constrained**.
