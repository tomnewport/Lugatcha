# Customs and Etiquette

The practical "how to behave" guide — everything a visitor needs to be
**understood and respectful**. Uzbeks are famously warm and forgiving of
foreigners' mistakes, but observing these norms earns real goodwill. The
underlying logic comes from [Daily life](04-daily-life.md): **respect for elders,
the primacy of hospitality, communal reputation, and Muslim-majority modesty.**

## Greetings

- **Open with *Assalomu alaykum*** ("peace be upon you") — universal, warm, and
  respectful across ages and settings. Reply: *Vaalaykum assalom*.
- **Handshakes:** Men shake hands, often with the **right hand**, sometimes
  placing the **left hand over the heart** as a sign of sincerity and warmth — a
  characteristic and lovely gesture. A two-handed clasp shows extra respect.
- **The heart gesture:** Touching your right hand to your chest while slightly
  bowing the head is a graceful way to greet, thank, or show respect — use it
  freely.
- **Men and women:** Many women, especially observant or rural ones, **do not
  shake hands with men**; a man should **wait for a woman to extend her hand
  first**, and otherwise greet with a nod and the hand-on-heart gesture. Don't
  initiate physical contact across genders.
- **Greet elders first** and with extra deference. Ask after someone's health,
  family, and work — greetings are a **string of well-wishes**, not a single word;
  the exchange itself is the courtesy.
- **Use titles and kinship terms:** address an older man as **aka**, an older
  woman as **opa**, an elderly man as **bobo**, an elderly woman as **buvi/momo**;
  add **-jon** to a name as an affectionate/respectful suffix (*Aziz-jon*).
  Default to the polite **siz**, never the familiar **sen**, with anyone you
  don't know well or who is older.

## Respect for elders (the master rule)

Age and seniority govern much of social interaction:

- **Elders enter, sit, speak, and are served first.** Stand when an elder enters.
- The **place of honour (*toʻr*)** — the seat farthest from the door — belongs to
  the eldest or most honoured. Don't take it unless invited; if you are the
  honoured guest you may be ushered there.
- **Don't contradict or argue bluntly with elders**; show deference even in
  disagreement.
- Offer and receive things from elders with the **right hand** or both hands.

## Being a guest (the heart of it)

Hospitality is sacred: *Mehmon — otangdan ulugʻ* ("a guest is greater than your
father"). If invited into a home:

- **Bring a small gift** (see below). **Remove your shoes** at the door.
- **Sit where directed** — usually on cushions (*kurpacha*) around a low table or
  raised platform. Avoid pointing the **soles of your feet** at people or food
  when sitting.
- **Accept tea and food.** You'll be offered far more than you can eat; this
  abundance is the point. **Refusing outright can offend** — accept, eat what you
  can, and leave a little rather than declining.
- **Respect the bread.** Never put **non** upside down, on the floor, or in the
  bin; tear it by hand (see [Cuisine](05-cuisine.md)). This is one of the most
  important taboos.
- **Compliment the food and the home**; thank the host warmly (*Rahmat*, *Juda
  mazali*, *Rahmat, toʻydim*).
- **Don't overstay or, conversely, rush off** — follow your host's cues. A
  half-filled tea bowl is courtesy (it means "stay, I'll refill you"), not a
  brush-off.
- **Offer to reciprocate** or host in return where appropriate.

## Gifts

- Bring something when visiting a home: **sweets, pastries, fruit, chocolate,
  tea**, or a small token from your country. Flowers are appropriate for hosts
  (give **odd numbers** — even numbers are for funerals/mourning, a custom shared
  with the Russian world).
- **For children**, small gifts/sweets are warmly received; children are doted on.
- **Avoid alcohol as a gift** unless you know the household drinks (see religion,
  below).
- Gifts are often **received modestly** and set aside rather than opened
  immediately; don't be surprised if a gift isn't unwrapped in front of you.
- Give and receive gifts with the **right hand or both hands**.

## Religion and modesty

Uzbekistan is **Muslim-majority but generally moderate and secular in public
life** (see [Society & institutions](02-society-and-institutions.md)). Still:

- **Dress modestly**, especially at religious sites, in rural areas, and in the
  conservative south. Cover **shoulders and knees**; women may carry a **scarf**
  to cover the head at mosques and shrines. Men: avoid shorts at religious sites.
- **At mosques and mausoleums:** remove shoes where indicated, behave quietly and
  respectfully, ask before photographing people praying, and follow posted/implied
  rules. Some shrines have separate areas or expectations for women.
- **Ramadan (*Ramazon*):** during the fasting month, be discreet about eating,
  drinking, and smoking in public during daylight out of respect, even though many
  people and most restaurants carry on.
- **Alcohol and pork:** available (Soviet legacy) but **never push alcohol on
  someone**; many abstain on religious grounds. Don't assume.
- **Be respectful about Islam and revered figures** (e.g. Imam al-Bukhari);
  religion is woven into identity even where practice is relaxed.

## Photography

- **Ask permission before photographing people**, especially women, elders, and at
  religious sites.
- Bazaars, monuments, and the **Tashkent metro** are now generally fine to
  photograph (the old metro ban was lifted), but avoid **military, police,
  government buildings, and border/security installations**.

## Conversation: safe and sensitive topics

**Welcome / warming topics:**

- **Hospitality, food (plov!), and melons/fruit** — reliable, delightful.
- **Family** — asking about (and sharing about) family is friendly and expected.
- **Uzbekistan's history and heritage** — the Silk Road, Samarkand/Bukhara,
  **Amir Temur**, **Ulugʻbek**, **Navoiy**, the medieval scholars (great for a
  tech audience: al-Khwarizmi → "algorithm/algebra"). See [People](03-people.md).
- **Sport** — boxing, wrestling, football.
- Praising the country, its hospitality, and its beauty.

**Sensitive — tread carefully, listen more than you speak:**

- **Current politics, the president, and the security apparatus** — best avoided
  or handled neutrally.
- **Islam Karimov, the Andijan events, human-rights issues** — sensitive recent
  history (see [History](01-history.md)).
- **The Aral Sea** — a real environmental and emotional wound; fine to discuss
  with sensitivity, not flippantly.
- **Relations with neighbours and ethnic tensions** (e.g. Uzbek–Tajik heritage
  questions, the 2010 events in southern Kyrgyzstan, Karakalpak issues) — handle
  with care.
- **Don't lecture** about how Uzbekistan should run its affairs; avoid
  condescension about "developing" status — there is deep, justified pride here.

## Body language and small courtesies

- **Use the right hand** for eating, giving, receiving, and gesturing; the left is
  considered unclean for these.
- **Don't point** with a single finger or the sole of your foot; don't show the
  soles of your feet.
- **Public displays of affection** between couples are frowned upon; modest
  behaviour is expected.
- **Keep your voice and temper measured**; open anger and confrontation cause loss
  of face for everyone. Politeness and indirectness are valued.
- **Beckon** with the palm down, not an upturned finger.

## Money, tipping, bargaining

- **Bargaining** is normal and expected at **bazaars and with taxis** (agree a
  fare first), but **not** in fixed-price shops, supermarkets, or restaurants.
  Bargain good-naturedly, with a smile.
- **Tipping** is modest: rounding up or ~10% in nicer restaurants is appreciated;
  some add a service charge. Tipping isn't deeply ingrained but is welcomed.
- **Carry cash** (small soʻm notes) for bazaars and small vendors; cards/apps work
  in cities (see [Society & institutions](02-society-and-institutions.md)).

## Quick do / don't summary

**Do:**
- Greet with *Assalomu alaykum*; use **siz** and kinship titles; greet elders
  first.
- Accept tea and food; compliment them; honour the bread.
- Remove shoes in homes; dress modestly at religious sites.
- Use your right hand; bring a small gift; praise the plov and the melons.
- Show deference to elders; ask after family.

**Don't:**
- Put bread upside down, on the floor, or in the bin; don't waste food.
- Push alcohol on anyone; assume everyone drinks (or that no one does).
- Photograph people (especially women) or security sites without care/permission.
- Use the familiar **sen** with strangers/elders; argue bluntly with elders.
- Lecture about politics; raise Karimov/Andijan/Aral flippantly.
- Show the soles of your feet; use the left hand for food/gifts; show public
  affection.

Get the greetings, the bread, the tea, the **siz**, and respect for elders right,
and you will be received with the warmth Uzbekistan is famous for.
