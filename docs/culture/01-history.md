# History

A working timeline of Uzbekistan and the lands that became it. History is alive
in Uzbekistan — the great Silk Road cities, the legacy of Amir Temur (Tamerlane),
the Soviet century, and independence all shape how people see themselves today.
Treat dates and figures here as orientation; verify specifics before relying on
them.

## Why history matters to a visitor

Uzbekistan sits at the **heart of the Silk Road** and at the crossroads of the
Persian and Turkic worlds. National identity leans heavily on a few pillars: the
ancient civilisation of **Transoxiana** (the land "beyond the Oxus", between the
Amu Darya and Syr Darya rivers, called *Movarounnahr* in Uzbek/Arabic), the
golden ages of **Samarkand and Bukhara**, the empire of **Amir Temur**, and the
journey through Russian/Soviet rule to **independence in 1991**. Knowing this
arc helps you understand national pride, monuments, money, and street names.

## Ancient and classical

- **Antiquity:** The region (ancient **Sogdiana**, **Bactria**, **Khwarezm**) was
  part of the Persian **Achaemenid Empire**, then conquered by **Alexander the
  Great** (4th c. BCE). Sogdian merchants became the great middlemen of the Silk
  Road.
- **Silk Road:** From roughly the 1st century BCE, the cities of the region
  thrived on the overland trade between China, India, Persia, and the
  Mediterranean. **Samarkand** and **Bukhara** grew rich and cosmopolitan.
- **Arab conquest and Islam (8th c. CE):** Arab armies brought **Islam**, which
  became and remains the dominant religion and a deep cultural layer. Arabic
  became the language of learning.
- **Islamic Golden Age:** Under the **Samanid** dynasty (9th–10th c., capital
  Bukhara) and successors, the region became a world centre of science and
  scholarship. Towering figures of this era — claimed as part of the region's
  heritage — include the mathematician **al-Khwarizmi** (from Khwarezm; "algebra"
  and "algorithm" derive from his name/work), the polymath physician **Ibn Sina
  (Avicenna)**, the scientist **al-Biruni**, and the great hadith scholar **Imam
  al-Bukhari**. (See [People](03-people.md).)

## The Turkic and Mongol turn

- Successive **Turkic dynasties** (Karakhanids, Seljuks, Khwarezmshahs) ruled, and
  the region's population became increasingly Turkic-speaking, layered over a
  Persian-speaking urban culture.
- **1220:** **Genghis Khan's Mongols** devastated the cities — a trauma
  remembered to this day.
- The name "Uzbek" itself is usually traced to **Öz Beg Khan** of the Golden
  Horde (14th c.); the **Uzbek tribes** later moved south into the region.

## The Temurid golden age (the national high point)

- **Amir Temur (Tamerlane, 1336–1405):** A Turco-Mongol conqueror who built a vast
  empire from his capital at **Samarkand**. He is **modern Uzbekistan's central
  national hero** — you will see his statues (replacing Lenin's after
  independence), his name on squares and institutions, and his mausoleum (the
  **Gur-e-Amir**) in Samarkand. Honour him in conversation; he is a source of
  enormous pride.
- **Ulugʻbek (Ulugh Beg, 1394–1449):** Temur's grandson, a ruler-astronomer whose
  **observatory** at Samarkand produced remarkably accurate star catalogues. A
  symbol of the region's scientific heritage.
- **Temurid Renaissance:** Under the Temurids, Samarkand and Herat flourished in
  architecture, science, miniature painting, and literature. This is when
  **Alisher Navoiy** (1441–1501) wrote the Turkic poetry that made him the father
  of Uzbek literature.

## The khanates and Russian conquest

- **16th–19th c.:** After the Temurids, the region fragmented into Uzbek-ruled
  **khanates** — chiefly the **Emirate of Bukhara**, the **Khanate of Khiva**
  (Khwarezm), and the **Khanate of Kokand** (in the Ferghana Valley). Famed for
  their architecture and, less proudly, the slave trade and intrigue (the "Great
  Game" era).
- **Russian conquest (mid–late 19th c.):** The **Russian Empire** absorbed the
  region, taking Tashkent (1865) and reducing Bukhara and Khiva to protectorates.
  Tashkent became the capital of **Russian Turkestan**.

## The Soviet century

- **1917 Revolution → 1924:** After the Bolshevik Revolution and the suppression
  of local resistance (including the **Basmachi** movement), the Soviets carried
  out **national delimitation**, drawing the modern republic borders. The **Uzbek
  Soviet Socialist Republic** was created in **1924**.
- **Soviet rule (1924–1991):** Brought literacy, industrialisation, women's
  formal emancipation, and urban modernisation — but also **Stalinist purges**
  (the reformist **Jadid** intellectuals were destroyed), forced collectivisation,
  suppression of religion, two **alphabet changes** (Arabic→Latin→Cyrillic), and
  the imposition of a **cotton monoculture** ("white gold").
- **The Aral Sea disaster:** Soviet diversion of the Amu Darya and Syr Darya
  rivers to irrigate cotton caused the **Aral Sea** to shrink catastrophically —
  one of the worst human-made environmental disasters, devastating the Karakalpak
  region's economy, climate, and health. It remains a live national wound.

## Independence and after

- **1 September 1991:** Uzbekistan declared **independence** as the Soviet Union
  collapsed. (Independence Day, *Mustaqillik kuni*, is the major national
  holiday.)
- **Islam Karimov (president 1991–2016):** The republic's first president, who
  ruled for 25 years. His era brought stability and a strong assertion of Uzbek
  national identity, but was marked by an authoritarian state, a tightly
  controlled economy, suppression of dissent and independent Islam, and serious
  human-rights criticism (including the **2005 Andijan events**, where security
  forces fired on protesters with heavy loss of life). He died in 2016. A complex
  and sensitive figure — approach with care in conversation.
- **Shavkat Mirziyoyev (president 2016– ):** Karimov's former prime minister, who
  has pursued **economic and social reforms and an opening up** — currency
  liberalisation, easing of border tensions with neighbours, expanded tourism
  (visa-free entry for many nationalities), and some loosening of state control,
  while remaining the dominant political figure in a system that is still far from
  a liberal democracy.
- **Today:** Uzbekistan is the **most populous country in Central Asia** (~37
  million people), young, urbanising, and increasingly open to tourism and foreign
  business, while balancing relations with Russia, China, Turkey, and the West.

## What to take away

- The **Silk Road, Samarkand/Bukhara, and Amir Temur** are the proud core of
  national identity — safe, welcome topics, and genuinely magnificent.
- The **Soviet legacy** is mixed and complicated; the **Aral Sea** and **Andijan**
  are sensitive.
- **Living presidents and current politics** are best handled with tact — listen
  more than you opine (see [Customs & etiquette](06-customs-and-etiquette.md)).
- Independence (1991) and the post-2016 reforms frame the country you'll actually
  encounter: optimistic, young, opening up, intensely proud of its heritage.
