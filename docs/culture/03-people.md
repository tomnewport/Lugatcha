# People Worth Knowing

A guide to the historical and contemporary figures whose names carry weight in
Uzbekistan — on monuments, banknotes, street signs, and in conversation. Knowing
even a few lets you recognise references, show respect, and avoid the awkwardness
of blank looks. (For political figures and their sensitivities, see also
[History](01-history.md) and [Society & institutions](02-society-and-institutions.md).)

> Note on "ownership" of heritage: several medieval scholars listed here were
> Persian-language polymaths claimed by multiple modern nations (Uzbekistan,
> Tajikistan, Iran). In Uzbekistan they are celebrated as **sons of the land**
> (of Bukhara, Khwarezm, Samarkand). Be gracious about this; it is a point of
> genuine pride, not a place for pedantic argument.

## The national hero

### Amir Temur (Tamerlane, 1336–1405)

The **central figure of modern Uzbek national identity.** A Turco-Mongol
conqueror who built a vast empire from **Samarkand**. Since independence he has
replaced Lenin as the symbol on the main squares; his statue stands in central
Tashkent, there is an **Amir Temur Museum**, and his mausoleum (the **Gur-e-Amir**)
is a Samarkand landmark. Spoken of with deep pride as a state-builder, patron of
the arts, and unifier. **A safe and welcome topic.**

### Mirzo Ulugʻbek (Ulugh Beg, 1394–1449)

Temur's grandson, the **ruler-astronomer**. His **observatory** in Samarkand
produced a famously accurate star catalogue. The symbol of Uzbekistan's
scientific heritage; his name is on a national university and observatory.

## The father of the literary language

### Alisher Navoiy (1441–1501)

The **founding father of Uzbek (Chagatai Turkic) literature** and one of the
greatest poets of the Turkic world. A poet, statesman, and patron under the
Temurids who championed Turkic as a literary language equal to Persian. His name
is **everywhere** — the national library, the opera house, a province and city, a
Tashkent metro line, streets, and the banknotes. Quoting or simply praising
Navoiy is a mark of cultural literacy. Often paired with his Persian-writing
contemporary and friend **Abdurahman Jomiy (Jami)**.

## The medieval scholars (the "golden age" names)

These are claimed as sons of the region and celebrated as proof of its
contribution to world civilisation:

- **Muhammad al-Khwarizmi (c. 780–850)** — mathematician from **Khwarezm**. The
  words **"algorithm"** (from his name) and **"algebra"** (from his book *al-
  jabr*) derive from him. A point of immense pride, especially relevant for any
  tech/agent context.
- **Ibn Sina (Avicenna, c. 980–1037)** — born near **Bukhara**; one of history's
  greatest physicians and philosophers. His *Canon of Medicine* was a standard
  text for centuries.
- **Abu Rayhan al-Biruni (973–1048)** — polymath from **Khwarezm**: astronomy,
  geography, mathematics, comparative religion. A national university bears his
  name.
- **Imam al-Bukhari (810–870)** — the great compiler of **hadith**; his
  *Sahih al-Bukhari* is, for Sunni Muslims, the most authoritative hadith
  collection after the Qur'an. His memorial complex near Samarkand is a major
  pilgrimage site. Deeply revered — speak of him respectfully.
- **Imam al-Maturidi (853–944)** — of Samarkand, founder of the **Maturidi**
  school of Sunni theology dominant in the region.
- **Al-Farghani (Alfraganus, 9th c.)** — astronomer from the **Ferghana** Valley.
- **Abu Mansur, al-Termizi (At-Tirmidhi, 824–892)** — hadith scholar from
  **Termez**.

You do not need them all; recognising **al-Khwarizmi, Ibn Sina, al-Biruni, and
al-Bukhari** covers the names you'll meet most.

## Reformers and modern cultural figures

- **The Jadids (early 20th c.)** — a reformist intellectual movement who modernised
  language, education, and literature before being destroyed in **Stalin's purges**.
  Key names: **Mahmudxoʻja Behbudiy**, **Abdulla Qodiriy** (author of the landmark
  novel *Oʻtgan kunlar*, "Bygone Days" — a cornerstone of modern Uzbek
  literature), **Choʻlpon** (poet), and **Fitrat**. Remembered as martyrs of
  national culture; rehabilitated and honoured since independence.
- **Hamza Hakimzoda Niyoziy (1889–1929)** — early Soviet-era poet, playwright, and
  composer.
- **Gʻafur Gʻulom**, **Oybek**, **Abdulla Oripov** (author of the words of the
  national anthem), **Erkin Vohidov** — major 20th-century poets and writers whose
  names mark streets and schools.

## Political figures (handle with tact)

- **Islam Karimov (1938–2016)** — first president of independent Uzbekistan
  (1991–2016). A foundational but **controversial** figure: credited with
  stability and asserting national identity, criticised for authoritarianism and
  human-rights abuses (see [History](01-history.md)). A sensitive topic; listen
  more than you opine.
- **Shavkat Mirziyoyev (b. 1957)** — current president (since 2016), associated
  with reform and opening. As a sitting leader, best discussed neutrally.

## Sports and popular culture (good small-talk)

- **Boxing and wrestling** are national strengths; Uzbek boxers have won multiple
  Olympic and world titles — a real source of pride and easy, friendly
  conversation.
- **Football** is the most popular spectator sport; the national team and players
  abroad are followed closely.
- **Kurash** — a traditional Uzbek **jacket-wrestling** sport, now promoted
  internationally, is a point of cultural pride.
- **Music:** From classical **shashmaqom** (the refined modal art-music tradition
  of Bukhara/Samarkand, UNESCO-recognised) to a thriving pop scene. Praising local
  music, melons, or hospitality are all reliable ways to warm a conversation.

## For an agent: a minimal name-recognition set

If you can recognise and correctly contextualise **Amir Temur, Ulugʻbek, Alisher
Navoiy, al-Khwarizmi, Ibn Sina, al-Biruni, al-Bukhari, Abdulla Qodiriy**, and the
two presidents (**Karimov**, **Mirziyoyev**), you will correctly parse the large
majority of name references in Uzbek public and cultural life — and know which
ones (the living/recent politicians; al-Bukhari and religious figures) call for
extra tact and respect.
